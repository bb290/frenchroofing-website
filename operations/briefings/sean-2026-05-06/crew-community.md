# Crew & Community

**For:** Sean
**The first of French Roofing's social campaigns. About people, on the job and off.**

## What this is

The bucket of posts about the human side of French Roofing. Specifically:

- The crew, on a job, in branded gear.
- Trucks parked on residential streets in Damascus, Happy Valley, Clackamas.
- Customers in front of their finished homes.
- Team moments — bowling night, morning safety check, end-of-day cleanup.
- Long-tenured crew members and their stories.
- Repeat customers. Multi-generational customers. Referrals from neighbors.
- Community involvement — sponsorships, post-storm response.
- The "doing it for your own grandma" ethic, made visible.

## What this isn't

- Stock photos of "a roofer" or "a happy family."
- AI-generated images of crews or customers.
- Close-up shingle shots (that's the Educational bucket).
- Pure before-and-after photos with no people (that's the Before/After bucket).
- Logo-only graphics with no humans.
- Generic "team appreciation" posts with no specific story.

## How a post sounds

The voice depends on who's in the photo:

- **A specific crew member by name.** Third person about them. *"Mike's been on the crew for six years and he's a better climber than I am at this point."*
- **The crew or company in general.** First-person plural ("we"). *"We did the Hendersons' first roof in 2018. This is their third house with us."*
- **A community moment.** First-person plural. *"Storm cleanup in Clackamas last weekend."*

Sean-only photos go to the Personal Brand bucket instead. See `personal-brand.md`.

## The first post

Brian giving a thumbs up mid-tear-off. The caption opens, *"Meet Brian. He loves tear-offs. The thumbs up isn't ironic."* Closes with, *"The crew's lucky to have him."* 55 words. The thumbs-up reference ties the words to the picture.

That post is the canonical example. Future Crew & Community posts should rhyme with its rhythm and length.

## Cadence

About one post per week. Same content goes to Instagram, Facebook, LinkedIn, and Google My Business.

## How we get the photos

This bucket is the one most affected by the photo bottleneck. We need consistent crew shots, job sites, and the occasional candid customer moment. Two sources:

1. **The quarterly photo day** captures a batch of crew shots in one focused session.
2. **Day-to-day on jobs** — anything candid the crew or you snaps and sends to me. Brian giving a thumbs up. A truck on a Clackamas street. The crew at the end of the day with the dumpster full.

The day-to-day photos are the ones we want more of. That's the consistency ask in the brief.

## Examples queued up

- Customer's handwritten thank-you card we received in the mail.
- Crew mid-tear-off, two-layer strip.
- Crew tying off safety harnesses before going up on a steep roof.
- Worker patching the deck before new shingles go on.

Each one is about 50–60 words. Each one is photo-forward. Each one closes with the standard call to action.

## Photo composition tips (when you or the crew shoot)

- Branded gear visible when possible (logo, shirts, truck).
- A face or human element. Even a partial counts (hands, back of head).
- Local context — Pacific Northwest roof, Damascus or Happy Valley or Clackamas street.
- Candid over posed.
- Natural light. Oregon weather is what it is. That's a feature, not a bug.

If a photo doesn't have a face, branded gear, or a recognizable local context, it probably belongs in a different bucket.
