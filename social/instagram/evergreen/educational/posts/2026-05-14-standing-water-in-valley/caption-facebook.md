# Caption (facebook)

Standing water in a roof valley is one of those things that looks harmless until it isn't.

Valleys are where two roof planes meet, and they carry more water than almost any other part of the roof. When debris builds up, leaves, twigs, moss, the water slows down instead of sheeting off. Slow water sits. Sitting water works its way under shingles over time, especially at the edges of the valley flashing where there's a seam.

We see this a lot here in the Pacific Northwest because we've got the rain volume AND the organic debris to feed it. A valley that drains fine in summer can back up by November when the fir needles start dropping.

The good news: if you catch it at the standing-water stage, it's usually just a cleaning. If it's been sitting long enough to lift shingle tabs or compromise the flashing, that's a bigger job, but most folks aren't there yet.

If your valleys haven't had eyes on them in a few years, that's a solid place to start.
