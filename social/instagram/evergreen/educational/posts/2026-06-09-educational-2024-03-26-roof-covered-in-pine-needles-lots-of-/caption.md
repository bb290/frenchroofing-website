# Caption

Pine needles look harmless. On a roof, they're one of the more stubborn maintenance problems we deal with in the Damascus area.

They pile up in valleys, along ridgelines, and in gutters faster than just about anything else. Trapped moisture sits underneath them, and that's where moss and algae get their start. Over time, that moisture works into the shingle surface and speeds up granule loss, which shortens the life of the roof.

If you've got big trees hanging over your house, plan on clearing debris at least twice a year, once in late spring after the cones and needles drop, and again in fall before the rainy season locks in. A clean roof breathes better, sheds water the way it's supposed to, and doesn't give moss a place to take hold.

You don't need to replace a roof just because it's got needles on it. You just need to stay on top of it.
