# Caption (linkedin)

Trees hanging over a roof look great from the street. From up top, they tell a different story.

Debris collects in the valleys, moss establishes in the shaded zones, and low-hanging branches abrade shingles down to the mat over time. The core issue is moisture retention. A heavy canopy keeps a roof wet significantly longer after every rain event, which is prime conditions for moss and algae to get under the shingles and start lifting tabs.

We see this regularly on properties in Damascus, Clackamas, and Happy Valley, especially older lots with mature firs and maples close to the structure. The roof itself may have years of life left, but unchecked tree overhang quietly shortens that window.

For property managers and homeowners alike, the preventive steps are straightforward: trim branches back six feet from the roof surface, keep valleys clear of debris, and schedule a moss treatment before it roots. Catching it early is the difference between a maintenance call and a repair bill.
