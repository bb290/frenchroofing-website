# Caption

Trees hanging over your roof look great from the street. From up top, they're telling a different story.

Debris builds up in the valleys, moss takes hold in the shaded spots, and branches rub shingles down to the mat over time. The bigger issue is moisture. A canopy that blocks sunlight keeps your roof wet longer after every rain, and that's exactly the environment where moss and algae get established and start working their way under the shingles.

We see this a lot in the Damascus and Clackamas area, where older lots have big firs and maples growing right up against the house. The roof itself might be in decent shape, but the tree situation is quietly shortening its life.

A few things worth doing now: trim branches back at least six feet from the roof surface, clean out debris that's collected in the valleys, and take a look at the north and shaded slopes for early moss. Catching it while it's surface-level is a much easier fix than waiting until the roots are lifting tabs.
