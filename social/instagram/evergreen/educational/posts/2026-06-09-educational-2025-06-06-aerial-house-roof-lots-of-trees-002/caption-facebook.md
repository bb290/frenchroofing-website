# Caption (facebook)

Trees hanging over your roof look great from the street. From up top, they're telling a different story.

Debris piles up in the valleys, moss takes hold in the shaded spots, and branches rubbing on shingles wear them down to the mat faster than you'd think. The bigger issue is moisture. A canopy that blocks sunlight keeps your roof wet longer after every rain, and that's exactly the environment where moss and algae get established and start working under the shingles.

We see this a lot around Damascus and Clackamas, where older lots have big firs and maples growing right up against the house. The roof might be in decent shape overall, but the tree situation is quietly taking years off it.

A few things worth doing now: trim branches back at least six feet from the roof surface, clear debris out of the valleys, and check the north and shaded slopes for early moss growth. Surface-level moss is an easy fix. Moss with roots lifting the tabs is a different conversation.
