# Caption

Here's something we see more than we'd like on older homes: flashing where the brick chimney meets the roof has started to fail, and water has been pooling at that joint long enough to cause real damage.

Chimney flashing is one of those details that does a lot of quiet work. When it's right, water sheds off the brick, runs down the flashing, and onto the shingles. When the seal breaks, or the step flashing lifts, or the counter flashing pulls away from the mortar, water finds a way in. And because it pools rather than runs, it doesn't need a big gap to do a lot of damage over time.

The tricky part is that chimney flashing fails slowly. By the time you notice a stain on the ceiling near the fireplace, the wood behind that brick has often been wet through a dozen rainstorms already.

If you've got a brick chimney, it's worth adding the flashing to your annual walk-around checklist. Look for gaps between the counter flashing and the mortar, rust stains running down the brick, or any lifted metal at the base. Those are the tells.
