# Caption

Most homeowners never see underlayment. It lives between your shingles and the roof deck, and it does a lot of quiet work.

Think of it as the backup plan. Shingles handle the bulk of rain and wind, but they're not a perfect seal. Underlayment catches what gets through, a wind-driven drip under a lifted tab, water wicking back at a seam, condensation that finds a gap. Without it, that moisture hits bare wood.

In the PNW, we run into roofs that were installed with minimal underlayment to cut costs. You usually don't notice for a few years. Then the deck starts to soften in one corner, and what started as a $500 repair becomes something much bigger.

When we install, we spec the underlayment for the roof's pitch and exposure. Lower slopes get heavier protection because water moves slower and sits longer. It's one of those details you'll never see once the job is done, but you'll feel it in how the roof holds up over the next twenty years.
