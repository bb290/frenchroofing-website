## Purpose

Live out the **Education** messaging pillar from BRAND.md §6: "roofing materials, storm prep, maintenance tips, insurance navigation." Teach homeowners enough to make informed decisions, even when the honest answer is "you don't need a new roof yet."

This pillar is what makes a stranger in Damascus think "those guys actually know what they're doing, and they're not trying to sell me something I don't need" before they've ever called.

The thing that distinguishes French Roofing's educational content from generic roofer content is **specificity**: Pacific Northwest climate, Damascus / Clackamas / Happy Valley local context, CertainTeed-specific material knowledge, and Sean's actual 20-year experience on roofs.

## What this pillar IS

- Roofing materials and what's actually inside a roof system (shingles, underlayment, ice & water shield, ventilation, flashing)
- Maintenance tips, moss, gutters, ventilation, attic temperature, debris
- Storm prep and post-storm assessment, what to check after a windstorm or heavy rain
- Warranties, what CertainTeed's manufacturer warranty actually covers, what French Roofing's standard workmanship warranty backs, what voids a warranty
- PNW-climate specifics, rain volume, wind, moss pressure, ice dam risk in higher elevations (Sandy, Estacada, Boring)
- Honest "you don't need a new roof yet" diagnostics, what to look for that means it's still got life left
- "Reading a roof," what we see when we look at one and why
- CertainTeed-specific product notes (warranty coverage, what the certification actually means)

## What this pillar IS NOT

- Stock photos of "a roof" or "a homeowner"
- AI-generated diagrams (unless explicitly approved on a per-post basis)
- DIY installation guides — we don't teach homeowners to install their own roofs because most of them shouldn't
- Lecturing tone — patient neighbor explaining over the fence, not a professor at a podium
- Anything that could come from any roofer's blog, content has to be PNW-specific or Sean-specific
- Pure marketing dressed as education ("educational" content that's actually a product pitch)
- Crew or community moments (those are `crew-community`)
- Sean's personality content (those are `personal-brand`)
- Before/after job comparisons (those are `before-after`, deferred to Kewl v2)

## Voice for this pillar

Use the brand voice from `.claude/BRAND.md` §4: down to earth, neighborly, light hearted. Tone shifts to **warm and patient** when explaining technical things (per BRAND.md §4 tone rules).

**Sentence rhythm:** Conversational, complete thoughts. Slightly more length is allowed when an explanation needs it, but tighter is still better. Target word count is **80–130** (vs. crew-community's 50–80), reflecting that an explanation needs a tiny bit more room than a moment.

### Person / perspective rule

The voice changes person depending on who's teaching.

- **Sean teaching from a specific moment** (he pulled apart a roof, he's looking at moss, he's pointing at a flashing) → **1st-person Sean** ("I pulled apart a roof in Damascus this morning and found..."). The differentiator from `personal-brand`: the post's **subject** is the lesson, not Sean. Sean is the teacher, but the focal content is the thing being taught.
- **Company-level teaching** (no specific moment, broader topic, no Sean photo) → **1st person plural** ("We see this a lot in Clackamas after a windstorm..." / "When we open a roof, the first thing we check is...").
- **No person at all** (close-up of materials, a tool, a damage example) → 1st person plural by default.

If the perspective feels ambiguous, default to 1st person plural. Sean's voice is specifically reserved for posts where Sean is the visible teacher in the photo or the moment is unmistakably his.

### Hook patterns that work

- **A specific moment as a teaching opportunity:** "Pulled apart a roof in Damascus this morning and found something worth talking about."
- **A question the homeowner won't ask out loud:** "Wondering if your roof can wait one more winter?"
- **A myth to bust:** "Most folks think a leak shows up where the water lands. Usually it traveled twenty feet first."
- **A heads-up framed for the season:** "Heads up if you've got moss starting on your north slope, here's what to do before the rain hits."
- **A material/spec note:** "Heard about CertainTeed's warranty? Here's what it actually covers."
- **An honest diagnostic:** "You don't always need a new roof. Here's how we tell."

### Avoid in this pillar

- Adjective-heavy claims ("amazing protection," "the best shingles," "incredible workmanship")
- Banned words from BRAND.md §4 (premier, industry-leading, world-class, etc.)
- Lecturing tone ("It is important to understand that...")
- Fearmongering ("If you don't replace your roof now...")
- Selling disguised as teaching (the post should be useful even if the reader never calls)
- Anything generic enough to come from any roofer's account

## Photo expectations

### What makes a good educational photo

- **Close-up of the thing being taught** — a shingle edge, a flashing detail, an ice & water shield course, an attic vent, moss on a slope
- **Material in context** — a shingle on the roof deck, underlayment partly installed, a vent boot mid-set
- **Damage examples** — wind-lifted tabs, popped nails, hairline cracks, blocked gutters, moss roots
- **Sean teaching** — Sean at a job site pointing at something, talking to camera, holding a sample. Extracted from quarterly video shoots
- **Natural light, real Pacific Northwest conditions** — a wet roof, a foggy morning, a rare blue-sky October

### What disqualifies a photo

- Stock or AI-generated
- Visible house numbers, license plates, full addresses (privacy baseline)
- Doesn't actually show the detail you're trying to teach about (too far back, too blurry)
- Shows a competitor's product as the reference (accidental cross-promo)
- Photo of a problem we'd never recommend (e.g., a poorly-installed roof that wasn't ours, where context could read as a callout of another contractor)

### Posting policy

First names of crew may appear without sign-off (per `crew-community/pillar.md`). Customer first names also fine. No street numbers, license plates, or full addresses in photos.

## Caption approach

Lead with a moment, a question, a myth, or a heads-up (see hook patterns above). The body explains the lesson with **one specific detail** — a year, a city, a material spec, a number. Not adjectives.

Close with a takeaway the homeowner can use. Not a sales pitch. The post should be useful even if the reader never calls.

**Social CTA** from `.claude/BRAND.md` §10 goes at the end, verbatim:

```
-
Visit website for Instant Roof Quote
Link in Bio
-
```

**No hashtags on Instagram captions** per `.claude/BRAND.md` §13. Plain captions only.

**No em dashes** per `.claude/BRAND.md` §4 punctuation rules. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or semicolons instead.

**Tagline use:** "Got You Covered!" only sparingly, max 1 in 6 posts in this pillar.

## Overlay text approach

Pull the lesson down to a single line of 6–8 words. Educational posts benefit from text overlays more than any other pillar because they carry information, but for v1 the OG endpoint does not render overlay text (per `crew-community/POST-EXEMPLAR.md`). Keep `overlay.txt` populated anyway, the v2 graphic upgrade (Claude → Canva, see `KEWL.md` v2) is expected to re-introduce overlay rendering and Educational is the highest-value pillar for that change.

**Good overlay examples:**

- "What's actually under your shingles"
- "Most leaks travel before they show"
- "Moss roots, not just moss"
- "Why we double-up the ice & water"
- "Wind damage you can't see from the ground"

## Frequency

30% of evergreen volume (long-term steady-state target, preserved through v1 even with Before/After deferred). At 1 evergreen post per week, this pillar fires every 3–4 weeks under normal photo flow.

## Common failure modes to watch for

- Posts that read like a blog intro instead of a caption (too long, too formal)
- Posts that any roofer's account could publish (not specific to PNW or Sean)
- Posts that turn into a pitch in the closing line
- Repeated topics, falling into a rut on storm prep or moss because those are the easy photos
- Lecturing tone creeping in when the technical content gets dense
- Mistaking "hard to install" detail (only of interest to other roofers) for "useful for homeowners" detail
- Forgetting the pillar's core promise: the homeowner walks away knowing something useful, even if they never call
